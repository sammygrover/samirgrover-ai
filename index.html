Thesis

I've spent my career studying how physicians develop competence — in endoscopy, in clinical reasoning, in the messy human process of learning to be a doctor. The question I keep coming back to is: what happens to that process when machines start getting genuinely good at the things we've been training humans to do?

AI can already detect polyps better than most endoscopists. It can read imaging, draft notes, synthesize evidence. The tasks we've built entire training programs around are becoming automatable. That changes everything — not just what we teach, but why, and what it means to be a physician at all.

I think we need to redesign medical education from the ground up — not to compete with AI, but to prepare physicians for a world where they work alongside it.
